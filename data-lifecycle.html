The Research Data Management Life Cycle and FAIR

The Research Data Life Cycle

The Research Data Management (RDM) life cycle describes how research data develops from its initial creation to its long‑term preservation and potential reuse. It emphasises that data is not static: it moves through stages of increasing stability, documentation, and governance. Although different models use slightly different terminology, four core stages are widely recognised.

1. Process

In the early phase of a project, data is created, cleaned, and transformed. Files exist as working copies on local machines or compute systems. They are volatile, frequently modified, and organised primarily for workflow convenience rather than long‑term stewardship.

2. Analyse

As the project progresses, files begin to stabilise. Cleaned datasets, model outputs, calibration files, and canonical scripts emerge. At this point, some files start to gain scientific identity and become candidates for long‑term retention.

3. Archived

Once results stabilise, selected files are combined with metadata and persistent identifiers to form digital objects. These objects are stored in preservation‑oriented environments and protected through fixity checks, replication, and retention policies. They may remain internal or restricted, depending on the project’s needs.

4. Published

A subset of digital objects are curated for dissemination. These objects are further enriched with descriptive metadata, assigned globally resolvable identifiers (such as DOIs), and made available for discovery and reuse. This is the point where data becomes part of the scholarly record.

Together, these stages form the structural backbone for understanding how data moves from working files to curated, citable digital objects.

The FAIR Data Principles

The FAIR principles - Findability, Accessibility, Interoperability, and Reusability - provide a framework for ensuring that research data can be discovered, understood, and reused by humans and machines. They are not a standard or a technology, but a set of guiding principles for good data stewardship.

Findable

Data should be described with rich metadata and assigned a persistent identifier so it can be located by humans and machines.

Accessible

Data and metadata should be retrievable using standardised protocols. Access may be open, restricted, or controlled, but the conditions must be clear.

Interoperable

Data should use well‑defined formats, vocabularies, and standards so it can be integrated with other datasets and tools.

Reusable

Data should be richly described, licensed, and documented so it can be reused in new contexts. Provenance and quality information are essential.

The FAIR Principles are about maximising the value and usability of data, not necessarily making it open. It applies to all data, regardless of access level.

How FAIR and the RDM Life Cycle Fit Together

The RDM life cycle describes the processes data goes through.
FAIR specifies the requirements for data to be useful to humans and machines.

A key insight is that the scope of FAIR changes as data moves through the life cycle.
FAIR is not something that “switches on” only at publication. Instead, it applies throughout, but for different audiences and purposes.

FAIR applies at every stage — the audience and purpose simply changes.

Research Data Management Services Across the Life Cycle and How FAIR Evolves

How do the FAIR principles map onto the stages of the Research Data Life Cycle?
And how can data infrastructure and services support these shifting interpretations of FAIR?

The RDM life cycle and the FAIR principles each describe essential aspects of data stewardship, but they operate at different levels. The life cycle outlines conceptual stages of research activity, while FAIR defines the qualities that make data valuable and usable. To connect these two perspectives in a way that supports real systems and services, we need an intermediate layer: data states. Data states translate conceptual stages into operational conditions that storage systems, metadata services, and governance workflows can act upon.

As research data moves from creation to long‑term preservation and publication, it passes through distinct data states. These states act as the operational link between the RDM life cycle and the FAIR principles:
- the life cycle describes how data changes,
- the data states describe how data is managed,
- and FAIR describes the qualities data should exhibit within each state.

Understanding these states clarifies which RDM services, storage environments, and governance mechanisms are appropriate at each point.

We can distinguish five operational data states:

  1. Active — data is being created and modified; FAIR supports internal organisation and team‑level reuse.
  2. Closed — data stabilises; FAIR begins to support institutional stewardship and future interpretation.
  3. Locked (Closed & Inactive) — data is no longer in use; FAIR ensures it remains intelligible and manageable beyond the project.
  4. Archived & Preserved — data becomes a curated digital object; FAIR enables long‑term discovery, integrity, and interoperability.
  5. Published & Preserved — selected objects enter the scholarly record; FAIR supports global findability, accessibility, and reuse.

Together, these states provide the practical structure that allows the life cycle and FAIR to align in real workflows and infrastructures.

The five states tie the life cycle and FAIR together: each life‑cycle stage corresponds to a data state, and each data state demands a different expression of FAIR.

Early in the project, FAIR is inward‑facing and pragmatic.
In mid‑life, FAIR becomes institutional and stewardship‑oriented.
At the end, FAIR becomes outward‑facing, enabling global discovery and reuse.

In this way, the data states form the conceptual hinge that connects the evolving nature of data (the life cycle) with the evolving expectations placed on it (FAIR).

FAIR Across the RDM Life Cycle

Putting it all together

The following sections describe how FAIR plays out in each data state and how that relates to each stage of the RDM Life Cycle.

Active — Process Stage — FAIR‑for‑Teams

Typical services: HPC scratch, project storage, ELNs, version control.

This stage supports rapid iteration and collaborative work. Files change frequently, so the priority is capturing provenance, i.e. records of how data was collected, processed, and transformed. At this point, provenance is often the only reliable account of what happened, when, and why.

FAIR here is practical and internal: clear naming, shared access points, open formats, and well‑maintained notebooks that help the team work reproducibly.

Audience: research team

Focus: efficient collaboration and early capture of provenance

Closed — Analyse Stage — FAIR‑for‑Teams transitioning to FAIR‑for‑Stewardship

Typical services: project storage with lifecycle rules, workflow engines, metadata templates.

The emphasis shifts from exploration to stabilisation. Cleaned datasets, canonical scripts and derived outputs emerge, and the provenance captured earlier is consolidated into structured forms. Workflow engines help standardise processes and record parameters, software versions, and transformation steps.

FAIR now begins to serve both researchers and stewards: files become easier to locate, access is controlled, and formats and vocabularies are standardised in preparation for long‑term retention.

Audience: research team + institutional stewards

Focus: stabilising data and consolidating provenance

Locked — Transition Stage —FAIR‑for‑Stewardship (Light)

Typical services: tape libraries, cold storage, retention systems.

The Locked stage acts as a buffer. Data is frozen in a read‑only state to prevent accidental modification while decisions about long‑term retention are made. Because the data no longer changes, provenance becomes especially valuable for appraisal and assessment.

FAIR here is minimal but essential: stable locations, read‑only access, and preservation‑friendly formats.

Audience: research team + institutional stewards

Focus: safeguarding inactive data and supporting appraisal

Archived / Preserved — Archived Stage — FAIR‑for‑Stewardship

Typical services: institutional repositories, preservation systems.

At this stage, data becomes a managed digital object. Files are packaged with structured metadata, assigned persistent identifiers, and placed in systems designed for long‑term integrity and accessibility. Provenance gathered earlier provides the context needed for appraisal, authenticity, and future reuse.

FAIR now operates at the level of institutional governance: persistent identifiers, controlled access, preservation metadata, and versioning.

Audience: institutional stewards and selected collaborators

Focus: long‑term integrity and contextual understanding

Published / Preserved — Published Stage — FAIR‑for‑Reuse

Typical services: public repositories, DOI registration services, discovery portals.

Selected digital objects are curated for dissemination. They receive rich descriptive metadata, globally resolvable identifiers, and are indexed in discovery portals. Provenance becomes critical for external users assessing quality and fitness for reuse.

Here FAIR reaches full scope: discoverability, standardised access, community vocabularies, licensing, and documentation that supports meaningful reuse.

Audience: global research community

Focus: discovery, citation, and reuse

Summary

This chapter began by presenting the life cycle and FAIR as separate frameworks; by following data through its states, we can now see how they converge into a single, coherent model of stewardship.

  • The RDM life cycle describes how data evolves from volatile working files to curated, citable outputs.
  • The states of a digital object determine which storage, tools, and governance mechanisms apply at each stage.
  • The FAIR principles guide how data should behave, their emphasis shifts as data matures:
    • FAIR‑for‑Teams in the Active and Closed states
    • FAIR‑for‑Stewardship in the Locked and Archived states
    • FAIR‑for‑Reuse in the Published state

Together, these elements form a coherent framework for managing data from creation to long‑term reuse.

This alignment sets the stage for the next step: recognising that what moves through these states is not just a collection of files, but a structured digital object.

As data progresses through the life cycle, it becomes increasingly clear that files alone are not enough. A file without context is difficult to interpret, impossible to trust, and often unusable. To be managed, preserved, and reused, data needs:

  • metadata that describes what it is,
  • provenance that explains how it came to be,
  • identifiers that allow it to be referenced,
  • relationships that connect it to other data, code, and publications.

In other words, data must be packaged and understood as a digital object, not just a collection of files.
This shift is essential for FAIR stewardship and long‑term preservation.

With the life cycle, FAIR, and data states now integrated, the concept of the digital object becomes the natural foundation for modern research data management: the primary entity that infrastructures recognise, preserve, and serve, far beyond the limitations of individual files.
