Definitions

Understanding research data requires a shared vocabulary. Before we can describe how data evolves, how it becomes a digital object, or how FAIR principles apply at different stages, we need clear definitions of the core concepts that shape modern research data management. These terms, Research Data Management, stewardship, governance, FAIR, reproducibility, form the conceptual scaffolding for everything that follows in this book.

The definitions below provide the foundation for that discussion. They establish the key ideas that will be expanded, operationalised, and connected to infrastructure design in the chapters ahead.

Research Data Management

Research Data Management (RDM) refers to the coordinated activities that support the creation, organisation, documentation, storage, preservation, and sharing of digital research data.
It ensures that data remains usable, trustworthy, and well‑governed throughout its evolution, from volatile working files to curated, citable digital objects.

In this book we align the with the idea that data moves through distinct operational states (Active, Closed, Locked, Archived, Published), each requiring different services, metadata, and governance.

Data Governance

Data Governance is the organisational framework that defines how data is managed across its lifecycle.
It includes policies, roles, responsibilities, standards, and decision‑making processes that ensure data is handled consistently, securely, and ethically.

In the context of the lifecycle, governance determines:

  • who can access data at each state
  • how metadata and provenance must be captured
  • which data should be preserved or published
  • how FAIR responsibilities are distributed

References

  • https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Data_governance

Data Stewardship and Data Stewards

Data Stewardship is the ongoing care and oversight of research data to ensure its quality, accessibility, and long‑term value.

A Data Steward supports researchers by:

  • advising on metadata and standards
  • ensuring provenance is captured early
  • guiding transitions between data states
  • preparing data for archiving or publication
  • ensuring FAIR principles are applied appropriately

Stewardship becomes increasingly important as data moves from Active to Closed, Locked, and Archived states.

References

  • https://web.archive.org/web/20220121022346/https://www.simplilearn.com/what-is-data-stewardship-article
  • https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Data_steward

RDM Life Cycle

The RDM Life Cycle describes how data evolves from volatile working files to curated, citable outputs.
Although models vary, the lifecycle can be understood through the four conceptual stages used in the next chapter:

  1. Process — active research, working files, rapid iteration
  2. Analyse — stabilising results, consolidating provenance
  3. Archived — digital objects under institutional stewardship
  4. Published — curated, citable outputs for global reuse

These stages correspond directly to the five operational data states (Active → Closed → Locked → Archived → Published) described in the following chapter.

References

  • https://doi.org/10.1145/1378889.1378998

FAIR

FAIR describes the desired behaviour of data and metadata so they can be discovered, accessed, combined, and reused by humans and machines.
It stands for Findable, Accessible, Interoperable, Reusable.

FAIR is not a binary property and not limited to published data.
In this book we explain how FAIR applies differently across the lifecycle:

  • FAIR‑for‑Teams in the Active/Closed stages
  • FAIR‑for‑Stewardship in the Locked/Archived stages
  • FAIR‑for‑Reuse in the Published stage

FAIRness grows as data matures and becomes more stable.

We will dive deeper into the relationship between the FAIR principles and the Research Data Life Cycle in the next chapter.

References

  • https://www.go-fair.org/fair-principles/
  • https://www.nature.com/articles/sdata201618

Open Science / Open Research

Open Science is a movement promoting transparency, accessibility, and collaboration in research.
It includes open data, open methods, open access publishing, and open educational resources.

Open Science intersects with FAIR but is not identical:
FAIR describes how data should behave; Open Science describes how research should be shared.

References

  • https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Open_science
  • https://book.the-turing-way.org/reproducible-research/open/

Open Data

Open Data refers to datasets that are freely accessible and reusable by anyone, typically under an open licence.
Open data is one possible outcome of the lifecycle—specifically the Published state—but not all FAIR data is open.

References

  • https://book.the-turing-way.org/reproducible-research/open/open-data/

Big Data

Big Data refers to datasets that are large, fast‑growing, or highly complex.
Their characteristics (volume, velocity, variety, etc.) influence:

  • storage choices in the Active and Closed states
  • compute requirements
  • metadata capture
  • long‑term preservation strategies

References

  • https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Big_data

Data Integrity

Data Integrity means that data remains accurate, complete, and consistent throughout its lifecycle.
It is protected through:

  • provenance metadata
  • version control
  • fixity checks
  • controlled transitions between data states

Integrity is essential for both stewardship and reuse.

Reproducible Research

Reproducible Research is research where results can be recreated using the same data, code, and methods.
Reproducibility depends on:

  • early provenance capture (Active/Closed)
  • stable digital objects (Archived)
  • transparent publication (Published)

It is a core motivation for FAIR‑for‑Teams and FAIR‑for‑Reuse.

References

  • https://book.the-turing-way.org/reproducible-research/overview/overview-definitions/
  • https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Reproducibility

Data Wrangling

Data Wrangling is the process of cleaning, transforming, and structuring raw data.
It is a central activity in the Process and Analyse stages and contributes to:

  • provenance
  • internal FAIRness
  • preparation for later stewardship

References

  • https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Data_wrangling
